Judas

Night had settled thick and heavy over the olive grove. The air in the Garden of Gethsemane was cool, scented with crushed leaves and damp earth. The moon cast pale light through the twisted branches, silvering the stones and shadows.

Earlier, He had asked them to watch and pray. But sorrow had pressed on their eyelids like lead. One by one, the disciples had drifted into sleep — confused, exhausted, not fully understanding the weight of the hour.

Somewhere in the darkness, footsteps began to multiply.

At first it was only a murmur. Metal brushing against metal, the low hum of voices carried on the wind. Then torchlight flickered between the trees. Orange flames broke through the blue of night, bending and swaying as a crowd pushed its way into the quiet garden. The stillness fractured under the sound of approaching boots and the clatter of weapons.

The disciples startled awake. Confusion flashed first, then dread. And then they saw him.

Judas.

He did not hang back in shame. He stepped forward. He approached Jesus as if this were any other greeting between rabbi and disciple. His voice carried the familiar word — "Rabbi" — and he kissed Him.

Betrayal wrapped in affection.

Fear surged. Peter reached for a sword, striking out in desperate loyalty. Steel flashed in the torchlight. Chaos erupted. But Jesus did not. He stood steady — no panic, no scrambling backward, no anger in His voice. He rebuked the violence and healed the wounded servant, restoring what fear had severed. Even now, mercy flowed from Him.

He stepped forward.

Whom do you seek?

And when they answered, "Jesus of Nazareth," He replied with authority:

I am He.

In that moment He could have called upon more than twelve legions of angels. He did not. Scripture must be fulfilled. The cup given by the Father would not be refused.

The ropes closed around His hands. The torches turned. The garden that had held whispered prayers now echoed with retreating footsteps as the disciples, shaken and afraid, scattered into the darkness.

But Jesus walked forward into it — calm and resolute, as though He were the only One in the garden who fully understood that this was not the triumph of betrayal. It was the unfolding of redemption.

Simon the Cyrene

The sun hangs low in the sky, casting a golden haze over the dusty streets of Jerusalem. As Jesus staggers forward, the weight of the cross crushes against His torn and bleeding back. Each step is a battle, His feet dragging through the dirt, His body trembling with exhaustion and agony. The wooden beam, rough and splintered, digs into His flesh with every faltering movement.

Around Him, a restless crowd swells — some weeping, some jeering, some watching in a silent, haunted awe. The Roman soldiers, clad in iron and indifference, bark orders and lash at His already broken body. But Jesus does not resist. He bears the suffering without complaint.

He falls — not in defeat, but in frailty. The kind of frailty He chose, so He could feel every ounce of what it means to be human. And in that moment, heaven watches as the King of kings falls, dropping the heavy cross.

Amid the chaos, Simon of Cyrene feels a rough hand seize his arm. The Roman soldier's command was not a request — it was an order. He was to bear the weight of another man's suffering.

Before him, Jesus stands, His body trembling, His face streaked with blood and dust. His eyes, though shadowed with pain, hold something deeper — something Simon cannot yet understand. With hesitant hands, Simon bends down, his fingers curling around the rough wood. As he lifts it onto his shoulders and walks beside Jesus, step by step toward Golgotha, something stirs within him. This is no ordinary burden. This is the weight of love itself.

If you are physically able, you may attempt to lift the weight of the cross before continuing.

At last, they reach the hill called the Skull — Golgotha — where the sky seems to hold its breath. The soldiers move with practiced efficiency, as if what they are doing is ordinary. As if it is not the hinge upon which all of history turns.

They strip Him and cast lots for His garments, fulfilling words spoken long before this day. They press a crown of thorns upon His head, each spike a mockery of the glory that is truly His. A placard is nailed above Him: This is the King of the Jews. They mean it as an insult. They do not know it is the most truthful thing written that day.

The nails are iron and cold. The sound echoes as they are hammered deep into His hands and feet. Jesus is lifted up between two criminals against a sky that was once split open at His baptism with the voice of the Father. Now there is only the jeering of the crowd, the weeping of women, and the labored, sacred sound of His breathing.

From the cross, He does not curse them. He does not call down fire. Instead, His cracked lips part and He speaks:

Father, forgive them, for they know not what they do.

— Luke 23:34, ESV

The hours pass like years. The sun, which had risen so brightly over Jerusalem just days before — when the crowds waved their branches and cried Hosanna — now withdraws its light. Darkness covers the land, as though creation itself cannot bear to watch.

And then, with every last ounce of love poured out, Jesus lifts His head and cries with a voice that is somehow still full of power:

It is finished.

— John 19:30, ESV

He bows His head. And He gives up His spirit.

The earth shakes. The great temple curtain tears from top to bottom — not from the bottom, as a man would tear it, but from the top, as only God could. The barrier between the holy and the broken is gone, rent open by the hands of grace.

A Roman soldier, hardened by years of blood and battle, stands at the foot of the cross and stares upward. Something moves within him that he has no name for. And quietly, almost to himself, he says:

Truly, this was the Son of God.

— Matthew 27:54, ESV

The Scourging

The air is thick with dust and judgment. Pilate's hands, freshly washed yet forever stained, tremble slightly as he motions with reluctant resolve. The crowd, a chaotic storm of voices, surges like a sea whipped by rage.

Soldiers step forward and seize Jesus. His eyes — pools of unspoken sorrow and divine resolve — scan the faces before Him. Not with fear, but with a mournful love that pierces deeper than any lash could ever reach.

Chains clink as they drag Him down stone corridors, the echo of fate ringing from wall to wall.

A coldness hangs here — not of temperature, but of cruelty. A wooden post stands at the center, scarred and stained. Hanging from the walls are whips with shards and leather cords soaked in anticipation.

Jesus is thrown against the post. His robe is torn from His back. He offers no resistance.

The guards ready themselves, their expressions blank. This is routine for them.

He does not cry out or beg for mercy.

He is silent.

Outside, the chants continue. Inside, the scourging begins.

His hands are tied high above His head to the wooden pillar, stretching Him until His back is laid bare to cruelty. The first lash comes with a hiss through the air — a sound that splits the silence like lightning before the storm. The whip bites deep, tearing through skin and drawing blood in thin rivers that spill down His sides. The soldiers do not tire; they strike again and again with leather tipped with bone and metal, designed not just to wound, but to destroy. His flesh opens like parchment, stripes written in agony across the back of the Lamb.

Mocking laughter echoes off the walls. Each blow is met with silence. No cry, no curse — only the steady breath of a Man choosing to endure. Pain blossoms like fire, and yet, within that inferno, Jesus stands. Not as a defeated man, but as a willing sacrifice.

Blood pools at His feet. The scourging ends, but the silence remains. The soldiers untie Him, laughing still, unaware that the blood they spilled this day will be the very blood that redeems the world.
